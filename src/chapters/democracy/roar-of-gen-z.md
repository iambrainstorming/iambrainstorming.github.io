# The Roar of Gen Z: From Protest to Complete Structural Reform

Date: 24-07-2026


Generation Z has finally taken to the streets. The streets of New Delhi have become the stage for a generational awakening. For years, young Indians were fed the "bread and circus" of corporate media, pacified by endless scrolling, viral reels, and the illusion of digital activism. But the facade has cracked. Gen Z have come to a profound realization: real, tangible change cannot be brought about through making reels, endless scrolling, or chasing likes.

Yet there is still a long way to go. Protest must also include questioning and, where necessary, boycotting media institutions — whether social media or mainstream media — that fail to serve the public interest.

Most importantly, this spirit of action must be sustained. A protest that lasts a week may generate headlines. A movement sustained for years can transform a nation. 

They must aim high. The role of Gen Z is not just to protest, nor is it to demand simple resignations. It is to demand the complete structural reform of our political and organizational systems.


## Beyond Tinkering: Questioning the System

Right now, a major rallying cry is to stop paper leaks in examinations. While stopping corruption is necessary, Gen Z must look beyond this symptom and question the disease itself. Are these exams even worth it? We have created an examination system that guarantees 99.9% failure rates, not because the students lack potential, but because the system is designed to filter them out. 

These tests do not evaluate actual skill, critical thinking, or applied learning; they merely test how much a student can rote-memorize. Demanding the cessation of paper leaks is just small tinkering. True reform means asking whether this archaic, memory-based evaluation structure should exist at all.


## The Historical Blueprint: Uprooting, Not Pruning

To understand the scale of reform required, Gen Z must look to the giants of history who did not settle for minor adjustments. 

They must be like Raja Ram Mohan Roy and Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, who did not merely ask for better treatment within the caste system, but completely uprooted the deep-seated practices of Brahmanism and untouchability. They must be like Mahatma Gandhi and Jawaharlal Nehru, who did not ask the British for minor administrative concessions, but threw out the colonizers entirely to give India its independence and draft a new Constitution.

Such monumental shifts are not achieved through small tinkering, like asking for a resignation or plugging a paper leak. They require complete structural reform.

## The Biological Imperative: Form Follows Function

To achieve this, we must understand the first principle of how life works: **Form follows function.** 

In biology, structure determines function. A bird’s wing is structured perfectly for flight; a fish’s gills are structured to extract oxygen from water. If you alter the physical structure, you alter its function. 

Human institutions operate on the exact same biological principle. We behave based on how our institutions are designed, how they are governed, and the structure of that governance. If the structure is flawed, the function will inevitably fail. Politics is not functioning well today because the underlying structure is broken. 

## Redesigning the Political Architecture

If we want different behavior from our politicians, we must change the voting method. The First-Past-The-Post (FPTP) system is arguably the most terrible voting structure we have. It forces polarization and partisanship. 

We must transition to **Approval Voting**. Under this structure, candidates are incentivized to behave independently and appeal to a broader consensus, rather than turning hyper-partisan to secure a narrow base. 

Consider the current absurdity of our political structure: a politician viciously abuses and condemns an opposing party, only to change parties through horse-trading. Suddenly, the new party is painted as a total utopia, while the old party is branded as pure evil. This hypocrisy is not just a moral failing; it is a direct result of a flawed structural design. 

To bring about further reform, we must introduce **direct democracy**. We need parallel institutes endowed with real power, allowing the public to bypass gridlocked representatives and directly pass referendums. 

## Organizational and Technological Liberation

This demand for structural reform must extend far beyond government. 

Let our organizations, companies, and institutions be run through [**Teal philosophy**](../organization/teal-organization-party.md)—embracing self-management, wholeness, and evolutionary purpose rather than rigid, top-down hierarchies. Let our schools be run by [School Management Committees (SMCs)](../education/why-some-schools-perform.md) utilizing this same Teal philosophy, decentralizing power and giving communities true ownership of education.

Furthermore, in the digital realm, every app we use must be [open-source](../programming/foss_philosophy.md). We must reject big tech corporate applications that function as surveillance malware, designed to track, control, and manipulate user behavior. The structure of big tech dictates a function of exploitation; open-source dictates a function of empowerment.


## Realizing the Constitutional Dream

Our Constitution asks us to secure Justice, Liberty, Equality, and Fraternity for all citizens. But these words cannot just remain ink on paper. 

We can only bring these ideals into practice by breaking the old structures and replacing them with new ones that actually function to deliver these promises. We cannot achieve 21st-century liberty using 19th-century political machinery.


## The Real Mission of Gen Z

The role of Gen Z is not simply to protest. It is not merely to demand resignations. It is not merely to stop paper leaks.

Its role is to imagine and build better institutions. 

Every generation faces a choice. One path is to repair cracks in an old structure. The other is to ask whether the structure itself needs rebuilding. History remembers those who choose the second path.

The first principle is simple:

**Form follows function.**

Just as biology teaches that structure determines what an organism can do, society teaches that institutions determine how people behave. If we desire different outcomes, we must design different structures. The challenge before Gen Z is therefore much larger than winning today's protest.

It is to build the institutions that will define the next century.
